How we cut alert fatigue 60% with behavioral correlation

Most SOCs do not have an alert problem. They have a context problem. Here is how a four-week tuning sprint cut a customer's daily alert volume by 60% without dropping a single true positive.

Priya AnandChief Threat Officer

The customer's analysts were closing 1,800 alerts a day across two shifts. Roughly 4% were true positives. Burn-out was real, hiring was hard, and the team had stopped looking forward to triage queues. The fix was not a new tool. It was four weeks of disciplined tuning.

The diagnosis

We pulled 90 days of closed alerts and clustered them by detection rule, asset, and disposition. Two patterns showed up immediately.

  • Eleven detection rules generated 62% of all alerts. None of them had ever produced a confirmed incident.
  • The same five service accounts appeared in 41% of "suspicious authentication" alerts. They were all known scanners, vulnerability tools, and backup agents.

The four weeks

Week 1 — kill the noise floor

We disabled the eleven low-yield rules entirely. For each one we wrote a one-line note in the runbook explaining why, so future analysts would not be tempted to switch them back on without context.

Week 2 — model the known-good

For the five service accounts, we built behavioral baselines: source IP ranges, target systems, frequency, time-of-day. Anything inside the baseline became a suppressed alert. Anything outside became a high-priority alert.

Suppression is not the same as deletion. The events still log; they just stop interrupting an analyst.

Week 3 — stitch the chains

We replaced 14 single-event detections with three chained correlations. Example: a single failed login is noise. A failed login followed by a successful login from a new geo, followed by a sensitive file read within 15 minutes — that is a story. The chain fires once with all three facts attached.

Week 4 — measure and lock

We re-baselined the queue. Average daily alert count had dropped to 720. True positive rate had risen to 11%. Mean time to triage dropped from 14 minutes to 4. We turned the new rules into the customer's official detection set and wrote a one-page runbook for each.

Why this worked

The work was not glamorous. It was reading rule logic, writing baselines, and having uncomfortable conversations about which rules were pulling their weight. The lesson we keep relearning: a SOC's effectiveness lives in its detection content, not in its dashboard count.
