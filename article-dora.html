DORA is here: a practical readiness checklist for financial services

The Digital Operational Resilience Act applies in full from January 2025. If your audit prep still looks like a spreadsheet of policy bullets, you are behind. Here is the checklist we walk every financial-services client through.

James O'ConnorVP, GRC Practice

DORA is not just another regulation to bolt onto an existing GRC program. Its requirements cut across IT operations, third-party risk, incident reporting, and board governance — and the depth of evidence regulators expect goes well beyond what most institutions have collected for prior frameworks.

What follows is the checklist we use to close the gap. It is grouped by DORA's five pillars, with notes on what auditors actually ask for.

1. ICT risk management framework

  • Documented ICT risk register with owners, treatment plans, and review cadence
  • Board-approved risk appetite statement, with measurable thresholds
  • Asset inventory tied to business processes, not just IP addresses
  • Defined recovery time and recovery point objectives per critical function

Common gap

An asset inventory exists, but it lives in spreadsheets that no one updates. Tie discovery to your CMDB or EDR feed and treat the spreadsheet as a downstream report, not a source of truth.

2. ICT-related incident reporting

  • Severity classification scheme aligned to DORA categories
  • Initial-notification, intermediate-report, and final-report templates ready to fire
  • Clock-driven escalation playbook (4 hours, 72 hours, 1 month windows)
  • Tested integration between SOC ticketing and regulator submission portals
The reporting timer starts when the incident is classified as major — not when you finish investigating. Tune your playbook accordingly.

3. Digital operational resilience testing

  • Annual basic test program covering all critical ICT systems
  • Threat-led penetration test (TLPT) every three years for systemically important institutions
  • Documented scope, methodology, and remediation tracking
  • Independent provider for TLPT scope

4. Third-party risk and concentration

  • Pre-contract risk assessment template covering criticality, sub-outsourcing, and exit
  • Register of information formatted to the EBA template
  • Monitoring program for critical providers, including SOC reports and incident notification
  • Documented exit strategy for every critical contract — not just a clause, an actual plan

Common gap

Most institutions can produce contracts. Few can produce a tested exit plan with timelines and migration owners. That gap is where regulators focus.

5. Information sharing

  • Membership in at least one trusted information-sharing arrangement
  • Internal classification of intelligence inputs (TLP markings, source reliability)
  • Process for actioning indicators in detection tooling within 24 hours

What to do this quarter

If you have to pick three priorities, pick these:

  1. Close the asset inventory gap. Without a defensible single source of truth, every other pillar wobbles.
  2. Run a tabletop on the 4-hour notification window. You will discover handoff problems on paper that you do not want to discover live.
  3. Draft a real exit plan for one critical provider. Use it as a template for the rest.

DORA rewards institutions that can show the work. The regulation cares less about which framework you align to and more about whether you can produce evidence on demand. Build for that.
