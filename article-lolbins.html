Hunting living-off-the-land binaries with UEBA

Native Windows binaries leave thin signal trails on purpose. Behavior-based correlation is the most reliable way to catch them — but only if your hunting hypotheses are precise.

Priya AnandChief Threat Officer

An attacker who lives off the land does not need to drop new files. They reach for the binaries already trusted on your endpoints — certutil, bitsadmin, mshta, rundll32, wmic, PowerShell. The signature world is mostly blind to this. Behavior, in contrast, is loud.

Here is how we structure a quarterly LOLBin hunt with the UEBA telemetry most mature SOCs already collect.

Step 1 — define the corpus

Pick a focused list rather than the entire LOLBAS catalog. We rotate among three monthly:

  • Download primitives: certutil -urlcache, bitsadmin /transfer, powershell IEX
  • Execution wrappers: mshta, rundll32, regsvr32
  • Discovery and lateral: wmic, net, nltest, dsquery

Step 2 — build the behavioral baseline

For each binary, query the last 90 days and compute a per-asset profile: invocation count, parent process distribution, command-line entropy, network destinations. Those four dimensions cover most of the signal you need.

The hunt is not "did this binary run?" The hunt is "did this binary run in a way it has never run on this asset before?"

Step 3 — score the deviations

Score each invocation by how far it sits from the baseline. We use four weighted features:

  1. New parent process for this binary on this asset
  2. Command-line entropy more than 2 standard deviations above the asset's mean
  3. Outbound connection to a domain not previously seen in this binary's network history
  4. Time-of-day outside the asset's normal activity window

Anything scoring above a threshold becomes a hunt candidate. We keep thresholds tight — false positives waste analyst time and erode trust in the hunt.

Step 4 — pivot from candidate to chain

A single anomalous LOLBin invocation is rarely the whole story. Pivot to the broader 30-minute window:

  • What user authenticated on this asset before the invocation?
  • What other suspicious processes ran in the same session?
  • Did the asset reach out to any new external IPs?
  • Were any sensitive files accessed in the same window?

If the chain holds together, you have an incident. If not, you have a candidate worth tagging for the next sweep.

What this catches that signatures don't

In our last quarterly review across managed customers, behavioral LOLBin hunts surfaced 23 incidents. Not one of them had triggered a signature-based detection. Six of those incidents were dwell-time longer than 30 days. Behavior catches what signatures cannot, but only if your hypotheses are sharp and your baselines are honest.

If you are starting from zero, pick one binary, build its baseline, and run the hunt for one month. Treat it as a learning exercise. The methodology generalizes; the artifacts you build the first time become the template for the rest.
